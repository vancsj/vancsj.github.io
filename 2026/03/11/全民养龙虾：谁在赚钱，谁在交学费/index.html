全民养龙虾：谁在赚钱，谁在交学费

发表于 2026-03-11 阅读次数：

2026 年 3 月，中国互联网上最火的词不是哪款爆红的游戏，也不是哪个明星，而是一只“红龙虾”。

OpenClaw 的 Logo 是一只波士顿龙虾，所以大家管调优 AI Agent 叫“养龙虾”。核心叙事很诱人：持续训练你的 Agent，让它从只会查邮件进化到能帮你自动监控竞品、打理业务。口号也响亮——“一人公司”：一个人 + 一群 AI Agent = 一家拥有整支团队战斗力的公司。

听起来确实是未来的样子。但在这场全民狂欢里，有一本账，大家似乎都不太愿意算清楚。

The Numbers First

In early March 2026, OpenClaw’s GitHub repository crossed 264,000 stars—reached in under 90 days, faster than React, Linux, or TensorFlow ever did. Tencent’s QClaw wrapper hit 100,000 deployed instances in a single day. Course vendors were selling “Zero-to-OpenClaw in 7 days, earn ¥100K/month” packages at ¥998 apiece, production cost ¥50, moving over a thousand units in three days.

2026 年 3 月初，OpenClaw 的 GitHub Star 数在 90 天内突破 26.4 万，速度刷新了 React 和 Linux 的纪录。腾讯的 QClaw 壳子单日部署量就破了 10 万。卖课的更疯，“0 基础 7 天精通，月入 10 万”的课程定价 998 元，成本几乎为零，三天就能卖上一千多单。

但普通用户的真实境遇呢？凤凰网对实际用户的调查显示，完成任务的平均月收入也就 500-800 元——接个十来单，每单也就几十块。扣掉 Token 费、电费和硬件损耗，这笔账根本算不平。热潮来得快去得也快，课程销量三周就跌了七成，“龙虾专用主机”也迅速从脱销变成了滞销。

这不是意外，这是每一波技术泡沫的标准剧本。

Who’s Actually Making Money

Three groups are actually profiting. First: course vendors and installation services. A single Taobao seller doing OpenClaw installs moved 3,000+ orders per month at ¥300–500 each—¥300–450K in monthly revenue. One college student earned ¥6,000 in a week doing house calls. Second: hardware resellers who rebranded ordinary mini PCs as “lobster-dedicated hosts” and marked them up from ¥2,000 to ¥8,000. Third, and most importantly: model providers and cloud infrastructure companies.

真正赚到钱的是三类人。第一类：卖课的和搞“代安装”的。淘宝上有商家靠卖安装服务月入几十万，还有大学生一周上门安装就赚了六千。第二类：倒卖硬件的。把普通 Mini PC 贴个“龙虾专用”的标签，价格敢从两千直接喊到八千。第三类，也是最关键的：大模型厂商和云基础设施公司。

钛媒体的报道说得很毒辣：“你养龙虾，大厂吃算力。”OpenClaw 软件虽然开源免费，但你跑它产生的每一笔 API 费用都进了大厂的口袋。OpenRouter 2 月的 Token 消耗量近乎翻倍，Kimi 20 天的收入顶过去一年。闷声发大财的，永远是背后卖铲子的人。

Classic shovel-seller dynamics: in a gold rush, the real money goes to whoever sells the picks and maps, not the miners.

卖铲子经济学就是这么运作的：淘金热里真正赚钱的，是卖铲子和卖地图的，不是淘金的。

The One-Person Company Myth

The “one-person company” narrative has a powerful origin story. In February 2024, Sam Altman publicly predicted: “We’re going to see 10-person companies with billion-dollar valuations pretty soon… there’s a betting pool for the first year there is a one-person billion-dollar company.” Chinese media ran with this. It became the intellectual backbone of the OPC movement—backed further by 20+ Chinese city governments with dedicated OPC policies, and Shenzhen’s “Lobster Ten Measures” offering up to ¥2M in grants.

“一人公司”这个叙事有个非常有力的背书：2024 年，Sam Altman 预言很快会出现“估值十亿的十人公司”，甚至第一家“一人独角兽”。这话传到国内，瞬间成了 OPC（One-Person Company）运动的精神图腾。再加上全国二十多个城市配套的专项政策，甚至深圳推出的“龙虾十条”，整个叙事看起来无懈可击。

But tools don’t fix business models. One-person companies have existed legally in China for decades. Their survival rate is under 10%—and that number predates AI entirely. The root causes are market positioning, commercial judgment, and execution. Automating your email doesn’t solve the problem that nobody wants your product. A Zhihu article said it directly: “The hotter OpenClaw gets, the faster one-person companies die”—the tool wave draws more competitors in, but doesn’t deepen any moat.

但问题是，工具救不了商业模式。在中国法律框架里，“一人有限责任公司”早就存在了几十年，存活率一直没高过 10%——这跟 AI 没半毛钱关系。一家公司的死活，取决于商业判断、市场定位和执行力。你用 AI 自动回邮件，解决不了“没人买你产品”的问题。知乎上有人说得狠：OpenClaw 越火，一人公司死得越快。 因为工具门槛越低，杀进来的竞争者就越多，你根本没护城河。

Who Actually Benefits

This doesn’t mean OpenClaw is useless. It means the benefit is unevenly distributed—and predictably so. Two groups genuinely benefit.

这不是在全盘否定 OpenClaw，而是说它的收益分布极度不均。真正能尝到甜头的是这两类人：

Knowledge workers with specific repetitive tasks. Product managers automating email triage, calendar sync, meeting notes. Content creators monitoring competitors, batch-formatting documents. E-commerce operators scraping pricing data. These people are solving “there’s a recurring task that code does cheaper than humans”—classic B-end ROI logic wearing a consumer product label. Their monthly gain isn’t ¥100K; it’s 20 hours saved and ¥3,000 in outsourcing costs avoided.

有明确重复性任务的“数字牧民”。 产品经理用它处理邮件、记会议摘要；内容创作者用它盯竞品、处理文档；电商运营用它抓数据。这本质上是 B 端逻辑：用更便宜的算力取代昂贵的人工。他们赚的不是那“月入十万”，而是省下的时间。

Developers. They build workflows, develop plugins, do localization work—acquiring real skills while building influence inside a fast-growing ecosystem. Bystanders, FOMO-driven entrants, and whoever bought the ¥8,000 “lobster host” are paying tuition, not making investments.

真正的开发者。 他们在写工作流、撸插件，在一个爆发的生态里刷级。至于那些因为 FOMO（错失恐惧症）冲进来、花了八千买台“龙虾主机”的人——他们不是在投资，是在交学费。

Shovel-Seller Economics

Every major technology wave follows the same structural script. The railroad boom of the 1840s: the real money was in steel and surveying, not train tickets. The dot-com wave: the winners were Cisco routers and Oracle databases, not most of the websites. The crypto wave: the winners were exchanges and mining hardware makers, not most of the token holders. The LLM wave so far: Nvidia.

每一波重大技术浪潮，都遵循同样的结构性剧本。1840 年代的铁路热潮：真正赚钱的是钢铁和测绘，不是铁路票。互联网泡沫：赢家是思科路由器和 Oracle 数据库，不是大多数网站。加密浪潮：赢家是交易所和矿机厂商，不是大多数持币者。这轮大模型浪潮到目前为止：英伟达。

OpenClaw’s wave is no different. The winners are compute providers and model companies. This isn’t a conspiracy—it’s structure. Open-source tools lower the barrier, expand the ecosystem, and concentrate consumption at the infrastructure layer. More users means higher API bills, which means more revenue for the platforms. Before entering, the question to ask isn’t “is this real?”—it’s: are you mining gold, selling picks, or buying picks? The return differential between those three is an order of magnitude.

OpenClaw 也不例外。赢家永远是算力提供商和模型大厂。开源工具通过降低门槛扩大了生态，最后把所有的消耗都导向了基础设施。入场前，你得问问自己：你是去淘金的，还是去卖铲子的，还是那个买铲子的人？

Raising lobsters is fine. The lobster can work—that part is real. But between the “¥100K/month” narrative and the ¥500–800 reality sits an entire ecosystem of anxiety-filling machinery: course vendors, installation services, government grant announcements, A-share concept stocks hitting daily limits—all of it helping you bridge a gap that shouldn’t exist.

养龙虾本身没毛病，AI Agent 确实能干活。但在“月入十万”的迷梦和“月入五百”的骨感现实之间，隔着一整套由焦虑驱动的利益链条。

The real question isn’t “should I raise a lobster?” It’s: are you doing it to solve a specific problem, or to avoid falling behind the times? The former is probably worth it. The latter is mostly paying the cloud bill for someone else’s data moat.

所以，入场前想清楚：你养它，是为了解决一个具体的麻烦，还是仅仅为了缓解“怕落后”的焦虑？ 前者大概率值得一试。后者，往往只是在替大厂的算力账单买单。
